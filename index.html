This quick win project was created as a way for our legacy software to have better navigation for administrative users and clients who will need access to the new platform.
The problem was an admin user had to go through many links to get to admin settings. Once there they had to navigate back to another tile page to have access to another area in the application. By simplifying this navigation (and making the nav bar consistent throughout) a user can now navigate the entire application from any screen. The resolution was to make all of our admin settings live in the header and make our app switcher up to general standards throughout the web. This will make usability easy on the transition. Icons, design styles, and naming conventions won't be changing, so familiarity and navigation will still be in place.

This is my process

Every project is different, so the process usually evolves and changes to fit a project's needs.

Give me information
Dump all the info you have on me. Tell me the problem users are having. Tell me what I am creating and the solutions it will have. User journeys and use cases if you have them already.

I do my research
This can mean a lot of different things but mainly what I do is ask questions to people who may have answers. People who use the software, and people who want to sell the software. People who train and know everything there is about the software. What works for them? What frustrates them? What are things they would do differently? How do they use the software (show me how they use it)?

Competitive Matrix and Industry Standards
That's right, I will sign up for free trials, watch demos, dig into their work, and see what works and what doesn’t. I have no problem looking at a popular piece of software and saying “yea that works” or “if we tweak this it would flow much better”

Compile all my info and start brainstorming
I gather user flows and how certain users may or may not use the software. How admins will see things and how regular users see things. I give them personas because that’s easier to talk to steak holders with. They can see user journeys much easier this way.

Sketch out everything
As of now I still do this on paper but I am dabbling with digitizing this process with Canva on an iPad and an Apple Pencil. I draw out all of my ideas. I add colors and scribble images, and texts. I see what may or may not work. And then I scan them and drop them in XD (or Figma). From here I can start filtering ideas down to what works and what doesn't.

Wireframing
I can usually go from low fi to high fi pretty quickly with a good library of components and a design system. It would be bad of me not to mention design for accessibility in the early stages. Nothing hurts me more than knowing someone would not be able to use something I designed. It's good practice to understand 508 and WCAG AA compliances.

Hi-Fi and Prototyping
Toss connecting lines to boards that need to connect. Animate them if needed. Anything to make the user experience as real as possible to stakeholders. The less complicated I can make it the more they get the big picture. Sometimes (often) I have learned it is hard for them to not nitpick dummy data. If you know that going in it can alleviate the problem with some pretty detailed mock options, they can focus on the task.

Testing and Refinement
It can be user testing with stakeholders on different options or more detailed AB testing on what works better. I really like AB testing but it is hard to do on larger features.
Make corrections to help solve the problem and go back to the testing/feedback cycle. Hopefully, this doesn’t repeat over and over. It is our job to understand the users' needs and not the sales needs or stakeholders' wants. Simplify it for the user. Take away the clicks and make software that a user can understand with minimum or no training.
